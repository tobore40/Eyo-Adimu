EYO ADIMU

Custodians of our culture and history in this part of the world have over the years been faced with a dilemma; how to improve, innovate, utilise modern technology and yet still keep our inherited secrets and norms intact.

The hierarchy of Awe and Eyo Adimu have thought long and hard over this and have come to the conclusion that the time is right, with the use of modern technology, to open up some of the customs and norms of the Adamu Orisa Festival to all who wish to know more about the festival around the world. We have a proud heritage and wish, in this 21st Century, to share it with all others. We hope it will put an end to the misinformation that abounds about this very unique event, the Adamu Orisa Festival.
This web site seeks to inform all visitors about the customs, norms and culture of the Eyo Festival and its rich historical heritage. If you have any inquiries, do contact us.

THE HISTORY OF EYO MASQURADE IN LAGOS

Categories

The word "Eyo" also refers to the costumed dancers, known as the masquerades that come out during the festival. The origins of this observance are found in the inner workings of the secret societies of Lagos. Back in the day, the Eyo festival was held to escort the soul of a departed Lagos King or Chief and to usher in a new king. It is widely believed that the play is one of the manifestations of the customary African revelry that serves as the forerunner of the modern carnival in Brazil On Eyo Day, the main highway in the heart of the city (from the end of Carter Bridge to Tinubu Square) is closed to traffic, allowing for procession from Idumota to the Iga Idunganran palace.
